The Submission of Prince Dipo Negoro to General De Kock (Oil Painting, 1830-1835)
Nicolaas Pieneman (1859)
Hendrik Merkus de Kock (date unknown)
Prince Diponegoro (1835)
Source #2
This source is an oil painting produced by Dutch painter and photographer Nicolass Pieneman, which demonstrates the power that the Dutch had over the Indonesian people at the time. In the image, the Indonesian people can be seen being surrounded by Dutch soldiers and looking up to Prince Diponegoro, wearing basic clothing, looking frail, and not having much. The Dutch, on the other hand, are clad in metal shoulder plates and belts, as well as thick boots, clean white pants, and fancy hats. They stand taller and hold weapons and are capturing the prince (who had fought and lead the war against the Dutch, which leads to his eventual capture and exile), suggesting the greater power that the Dutch soldiers had. This image is important, as the submission of Diponegoro triggered the end of the Java war between the Dutch and Indonesian forces. The Java war, beginning in 1825, was caused by the Dutch attempting to pave a road over Diponegoro’s parents’ tombs. Leading the war was Diponegoro and De Kock, until the time depicted in this source, where Diponegoro was tricked by a false offer for a ceasefire and captured by the Dutch. This source is likely to be one of the most accurate depictions from the war, as the power that the Dutch had over the Indonesians made it difficult for artifacts to be created by the Indonesians. However, this image is likely biased to paint the Dutch as superior or better than the native people, as the creator of this source was a Dutch painter. Such biases could include not showing the casualties or damages done as part of the war.
